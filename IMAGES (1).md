## Full Catalogue

### LANDING PAGE / INTRODUCTION

---

**`cover_narrative_textile.jpg`** *(DP227595.jpg)*

An embroidered cotton textile depicting multiple Ramayana scenes simultaneously: battle scenes at top, a diagonal blue river cutting across the center, monkey warriors, archers hunting a deer, a building representing Lanka at lower right, Sita visible at top right, and dozens of figures from various episodes throughout.

- **Use:** Landing page hero image — shows the whole epic at once, giving readers an immediate sense of the story's scope
- **Style:** Indian embroidered pictorial textile, probably Gujarat or Rajasthan, 19th century

---

**`intro_valmiki_writing.jpg`** *(Valmiki_Ramayana.jpg)*

A seated sage with a white beard and saffron robes, writing on a manuscript resting in his lap, under a green flowering tree. Calm, devotional mood. This is Valmiki — the Adi-Kavi, the "first poet" — composing the Ramayana.

- **Use:** Landing page alongside the intro text; also the Book I (Bala Kanda) section header, specifically at Cantos II–III where Brahma commands Valmiki to compose the poem
- **Style:** Indian miniature painting, 19th century

---

**`intro_manuscript_page.jpg`** *(20_DP238218r2_61B.jpg)*

An illuminated manuscript page with two columns of dense Sanskrit text in black ink flanking a central rectangular miniature of a deity in a golden shrine with foliage. The borders are decorated in gold and red.

- **Use:** Introduction section — illustrates the manuscript tradition; works well as a decorative visual break between sections
- **Style:** Illustrated Sanskrit manuscript, 11th–13th century

---

**`intro_vishnu_shesha.jpg`** *(DP-22779-001.jpg)*

A chromolithograph showing Vishnu (blue-skinned, four-armed) reclining on the cosmic serpent Shesha on the primordial ocean. Lakshmi sits beside him. From Vishnu's navel rises a lotus bearing four-headed Brahma. Bengali text at bottom: "Shri Narayaner Anantashayana."

- **Use:** Introduction — establishes the cosmic frame: Rama is Vishnu's seventh avatar. Also directly relevant to Book I, Canto XIV ("Ravan Doomed") when the gods petition Vishnu to incarnate
- **Style:** Chromolithograph, Calcutta Art Studio, late 19th century

---

**`intro_vishnu_narayana.jpg`** *(DP-25009-001.jpg)*

A Ravi Varma Press chromolithograph showing a pale deity (Shesha Narayana or Balarama) seated on a five-hooded cobra canopy, flanked by two attendants in red saris, on water. Calm and devotional.

- **Use:** Introduction alongside `intro_vishnu_shesha.jpg` — both convey the divine world from which Rama descends
- **Style:** Ravi Varma Press chromolithograph, Karla/Lonavla, c. 1900

---

**`intro_vishnu_hanuman.jpg`** *(DP-37142-001.jpg)*

A chromolithograph showing blue Vishnu on the multi-headed Shesha serpent, four-headed Brahma on a lotus above, Lakshmi attending — and, notably, Hanuman in devotional posture to the left, with celestial musicians surrounding. This is the only Vishnu image in the collection that also shows Hanuman.

- **Use:** Introduction section — this image bridges the cosmic Vishnu narrative and the Ramayana story; Hanuman's presence signals Rama's world specifically
- **Style:** Chromolithograph, Shri Vaishnava Chitra Prakashan, Mumbai, c. 1900–1910

### BOOK I — BALA KANDA (Childhood)

---

**`book1_dasharatha_sage.jpg`** *(DP153156.jpg)*

A Mughal-style miniature in a multi-panel palace architectural setting. Central scene: a crowned king (Dasharatha) kneeling before an ascetic sage on a terrace. Multiple smaller simultaneous scenes visible in adjacent chambers — women watching from a latticed balcony, a figure reclining in another room, attendants below. Clean white Mughal architecture with salmon border.

- **Use:** Book I header — captures the central dynamic of the book: the royal court world receiving the forest world. Works for Cantos IX–X (Rishyashringa invited to perform the sacrifice) or Cantos XX–XXIII (Vishwamitra's visit demanding Rama)
- **Canto mapping:** Cantos IX–X or XX–XXIII
- **Style:** Mughal miniature, late 17th–early 18th century

### BOOK III — ARANYA KANDA (The Forest)

---

**`book3_kabandha.jpg`** *(DP-19336-001.jpg)*

A horizontal composition: Rama (left, dark violet/purple, crowned, bow drawn) and Lakshmana (smaller, red dhoti, also drawing bow) both firing arrows at a monstrous demon on the right. The demon is enormous — dark blue-gray, with an incredibly long arm stretched horizontally toward the brothers — already hit by multiple arrows. Reed grass in the background right.

- **Use:** Book III header — the encounter with Kabandha (Cantos LXX–LXXIV), the demon with arms of enormous reach who, dying, directs Rama to seek Sugriva. The long-arm demon is visually unmistakable as Kabandha
- **Canto mapping:** Book III, Cantos LXX–LXXIV (Kabandha)
- **Style:** Indian miniature, Pahari or Rajasthani, 17th–18th century
- **Also use as:** `images/inline/book3_kabandha_inline.jpg` for inline placement at those cantos

---

**`book3_ravana_sculpture.jpg`** *(DP317505.jpg)*

A green-bronze sculpture of a figure seated in deep meditation, with ten heads arranged in a crown-cluster and twenty arms spreading wide in all directions, each hand in a different gesture. The face is serene — this is Ravana as a supreme yogic master, not a monster.

- **Use:** Inline illustration at Book III, Canto XXXI (the first introduction of Ravana into the narrative). The sculpture's serenity conveys the crucial paradox: Ravana has genuine spiritual power and learning. He is terrifying because of what he chose to do with it, not because he is merely evil
- **Medium:** Bronze, Southeast Asian (Cambodian or Thai), 10th–12th century
- **Style:** Sculpture — works beautifully as a visual contrast against the flat miniature paintings

---

### BOOK IV — KISHKINDHA KANDA (The Monkey Kingdom)

---

**`book4_rama_lakshmana_night.jpg`** *(DP153185.jpg)*

Two warriors seated on a large rounded rock under a full moon and star-scattered night sky. Left figure (Lakshmana, with bow) faces the right figure (Rama, dark blue, with bow). Both are still, watchful. Two stylized trees frame the scene. The orange/red border gives warmth against the deep blue-gray night. The mood is quiet, patient, melancholy — two brothers waiting in the dark.

- **Use:** Book IV header — Rama and Lakshmana waiting on Mount Prasravana through four months of monsoon while Sugriva delays (Cantos XXV–XXXII). This image captures the emotional tone of Book IV's middle section perfectly
- **Canto mapping:** Book IV, Cantos XXV–XXXII
- **Style:** Indian miniature, Pahari school, 18th century

---

**`book4_monkey_forest.jpg`** *(DP153189.jpg)*

A panoramic landscape showing hilly forest terrain with multiple simultaneous scenes: monkeys active on hillsides, a figure meditating in a cave upper-left (possibly Sugriva in hiding at Rishyamukha), a thatched hermitage hut at center, a large dark rectangular structure with fire and thick black smoke in the foreground, and a palace visible in a cave at right. Blue sky above.

- **Use:** Book IV inline illustration — the multiple simultaneous scenes mirror the Kishkindha Kanda's parallel narrative structure (Sugriva in hiding, sages in the forest, the search parties departing in all directions)
- **Style:** Mughal or Deccani miniature, 17th–18th century

---

### BOOK V — SUNDARA KANDA (The Beautiful Book)

---

**`book5_hanuman_ravana_court.jpg`** *(DP107191.jfif)*

A vibrant Mughal miniature. Upper half: Ravana and his demon courtiers seated on an elevated platform under a red and green canopy — colorful demons in animated discussion, some alarmed, some aggressive. Lower half: Hanuman (the monkey figure) stands surrounded by a crowd in and around a body of water, the assembly watching. Extraordinary color and energy — the whole painting crackles with confrontation.

- **Use:** Book V header — Hanuman captured and brought before Ravana, delivering his bold speech (Cantos XLIX–LI). This is the Sundara Kanda's dramatic turning point: Hanuman, the prisoner, becomes the one lecturing the king
- **Canto mapping:** Book V, Cantos XLIX–LIII (Hanuman before Ravana)
- **Style:** Mughal miniature, late 16th–early 17th century

---

### BOOK VI — YUDDHA KANDA (The War)

---

**`book6_ravana_war_council.jpg`** *(DP107192.jpg)*

A two-register Mughal miniature. Upper register: Ravana (clearly multi-headed, in a rich red and blue robe) on his throne in an ornate blue-and-gold court, gesturing to a counselor. Lower register: an extraordinary procession of individual demon faces — dozens of distinct demon soldiers, each face different in color, expression, and character — filling the entire lower half of the painting.

- **Use:** Book VI header — Ravana's war council (Cantos VI–XVI). The lower register of individualized demon faces is one of the most visually arresting things in the entire collection. It makes Ravana's army real: these are not abstract evil forces but countless specific individuals
- **Canto mapping:** Book VI, Cantos VI–XVI
- **Style:** Mughal miniature, late 16th–early 17th century

---

**`book6_vibhishana_arrives.jpg`** *(DP103079.jpg)*

A Mughal miniature showing Rama (blue-skinned, seated inside a large red-brick domed pavilion, armed with bow) looking down at figures gathered below. Monkeys and human-like figures are assembled at the base, looking up. Two attendants stand at the pavilion entrance. Exceptional architectural detail: red brick, blue and gold trim, pointed arches.

- **Use:** Book VI inline illustration — Vibhishana's arrival and Rama's acceptance of him (Cantos XVII–XIX). Rama in his pavilion, the mixed assembly gathered below, perfectly captures the moment of the defector being welcomed into Rama's alliance
- **Canto mapping:** Book VI, Cantos XVII–XIX
- **Style:** Mughal miniature, late 16th–early 17th century

---

### BOOK VII — UTTARA KANDA (The Aftermath)

---

**`book7_rama_enthroned.jpg`** *(DP152344.jpg — use this, delete DP152344__1_.jpg)*

A folk-style devotional painting — flat, bold, warm palette. Rama with a golden halo sits enthroned at center. Female attendants flank him. Hanuman (small monkey figure) kneels in devotion at lower left. Two decorated pot-vessels at the base. The style is vernacular, popular — the image that would hang in an ordinary home, not a royal court.

- **Use:** Book VII header — Rama's coronation and the establishment of Rama-Rajya. The folk style is appropriate: Rama's reign belongs to the people, not just the court
- **Style:** Indian folk/popular painting, likely Rajasthan or Madhya Pradesh, 18th–19th century

---

**`book7_rama_court_ravi_varma.jpg`** *(DP702158.jpg)*

A Ravi Varma Press chromolithograph titled "Uttara Ramacharit" (Devanagari text at bottom). Rama (blue, in full royal regalia) enthroned with Sita beside him. Lakshmana behind with the royal umbrella. Bharata standing right. Hanuman prostrate at Rama's feet. To the far left: white-bearded Valmiki presenting a manuscript — the Ramayana itself. A small child (Lava or Kusha) sits near Sita's feet.

- **Use:** The single most narratively complete image in the collection — it depicts the entire epic's resolution in one frame: the family reunited, Hanuman's eternal devotion, and the poet presenting his work to the king who is its subject. Use as the closing image of the whole site
- **Canto mapping:** Book VII — the coronation and Valmiki presenting Lava and Kusha at court
- **Style:** Ravi Varma Press chromolithograph, Karla/Lonavla, Maharashtra, c. 1895–1905
